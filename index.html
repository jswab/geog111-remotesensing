Australia's "Red Centre" Turns Green

NASA Earth Observatory · March 2026 · Story by Kathryn Hansen

The town of Alice Springs lies near Australia's geographic centre, in a region called the "Red Centre" for the rusty hue of its desert — the result of millions of years of iron-oxide accumulation in the soil.

After weeks of heavy rainfall in February and March 2026, vast areas of desert and surrounding mountains turned lush and green. The Todd River and nearby riverbeds — normally dry for most of the year — flooded rapidly, submerging roads and vehicles across Alice Springs.

239 mm rainfall in Feb 2026 3rd wettest February since 1900 Tropical Cyclone Narelle

The Northern Territory received an average of 239 millimetres (9 inches) of rainfall in February — the third-wettest February on record since 1900, according to the Bureau of Meteorology. A vast humid air mass, partly driven by Tropical Cyclone Narelle, pushed deep into the continent's arid interior.

When water reaches the desert, dormant seeds and root systems spring to life within days. The green flush visible in this MODIS image is largely spinifex grass, mulga shrubs, and desert wildflowers. The dark sinuous channels are the flooded Todd River and its tributaries.

NASA's MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer) on the Terra satellite captured this image on March 10, 2026. A comparison image from January 2026 shows the same area as almost entirely red-orange desert.
